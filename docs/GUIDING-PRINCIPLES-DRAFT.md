# Niagara Queer Archive — Guiding Principles

**Status: Discussion draft — v0.1, 8 July 2026**

> This is a *starting point*, not a rulebook. It exists to give us something
> concrete to react to, dis/agree with, and shape together. Nothing here is
> settled. The values and structure are ours to make as a group, and this draft
> tries to pose those questions honestly rather than answer them for you.
>
> Everything below is written so it can be read by a newcomer with no context on
> the project. If a section reads as obvious to us but wouldn't to a stranger,
> that's a sign it's doing its job.

---

## 0. Why write this down

The Niagara Queer Archive began as a small group project and has, so far, been
built by one person with help from a small number of trusted friends. That's a
fragile way to hold something that belongs, by its nature, to a whole community.

Writing down how we work does three things:

1. **Protects the community.** People are trusting us with their stories, and
   sometimes with painful or private ones. They deserve to know how we'll treat
   those stories before they hand them over.
2. **Protects the archive.** If contentious questions come up — and they will —
   we want to have decided *how* we decide *before* we're in the middle of one.
3. **Makes it possible for others to join.** You can't invite people into
   something that only lives in one person's head.

This document is the "how we work" that a good community project needs, so that
maintaining the archive isn't dependent on any single person, and so that anyone
considering getting involved can see what they'd be joining.

## 1. Purpose & Objectives

### What NQA is for

The Niagara Queer Archive exists to catalogue, curate, and preserve the histories
of LGBTQ2S+ people across the Niagara region — including the small towns, suburbs,
and rural communities that are usually left out of big-city queer narratives — and
to make those histories visible, accessible, and connected.

We work from **direct sources**: local press, firsthand accounts, organizational
records, and materials contributed by community members. We treat queer history as
*living history* — not a closed record of the past, but an ongoing, growing thing
that people are still adding to.

### What NQA is *not*

Naming this matters as much as naming what we are:

- **Not a rumour mill or an outing tool.** We do not publish claims about someone's
  identity or private life that aren't sourced and consented. (See §3.)
- **Not a national or generic queer archive.** We stay rooted in Niagara. National
  wire/syndicated coverage is out of scope; Niagara mastheads are in.
- **Not a substitute for the communities it documents.** The archive serves the
  community; it doesn't speak *for* it or claim to represent it.
- **Not neutral about the people in it.** We center queer and Two-Spirit lives.
  Allies and partner organizations are welcome and valued, but they are framed
  clearly as allies — not folded in as though they were queer organizations.
- **Not a finished thing.** Gaps are expected and named openly, especially where the
  digital record runs out (pre-1980s life, the AIDS-crisis human toll, lesbian and
  women's-music collectives, etc.).

### Near-term objectives (open to revision)

- Move from a seeded first round of records toward **community-contributed** depth,
  now that easy digital source-mining is near-saturated.
- Build the **intake relationships** that unlock institutional and personal holdings
  (Fort Erie Museums, PFLAG Niagara, Positive Living Niagara, OUTniagara, area
  museums and Brock Archives).
- Establish a **sustainable way to run the archive** that doesn't depend on one
  person (this document is step one).

> **Open question:** What are our objectives for the *next year* specifically, and
> how will we know if we've met them?

## 2. Community Engagement & Participation

An archive of a community should be *accountable to* and *shaped by* that community
— not maintained behind a closed door. This is the principle we most want to get
right, and it's informed directly by watching how *not* to do it: closed groups,
narrow membership, little outreach or opportunity for the wider community to
participate.

### Ways to get involved

- **Contribute records** — share a story, photo, document, or memory through the
  Tell Your Story intake, or point us toward a source we've missed.
- **Research & cataloguing** — help find, verify, and write up entries from direct
  sources.
- **Outreach** — help build relationships with community members and institutions;
  spread the word.
- **Social media** — create and manage content for the existing
  @NiagaraQueerArchive Instagram and Facebook.
- **Email & admin** — help manage the inbox, respond to contributors, keep intake
  moving.
- **Events** — help organize the monthly storytelling series and other gatherings.

### Principles for participation

- **Openness by default.** We actively invite participation rather than waiting to
  be asked. Barriers to getting involved should be as low as we can make them.
- **Diversity is a requirement, not a bonus.** The people helping run the archive
  should reflect the breadth of the community it documents — across identity, age,
  municipality, and background. If the working group is homogenous, that's a problem
  to fix, not a detail.
- **Credit and consent for contributors.** People who contribute materials decide
  how they're credited (by name, anonymously, or withheld) and can change their mind.
- **Care over speed.** No contribution is worth publishing before it's been handled
  with the consent and sourcing care in §3.

### The student-labour question (open)

The May 2025 notes raised whether to recruit free student labour (e.g. co-op or
practicum placements). This deserves a deliberate answer because it sits right on
top of NQA's values:

- *For:* real learning opportunities, capacity we otherwise lack, connection to
  local institutions.
- *Against:* unpaid work can exploit exactly the young/precarious people we claim to
  serve; "free labour" and "community values" sit uneasily together.

> **Open question:** Do we accept student/volunteer labour? If so, under what
> conditions — supervision, real learning outcomes, credit, a cap on hours, a path
> to paid work if funding appears? Do we treat paid and unpaid contributors
> differently in how they're recognized?

## 3. Ethical Stewardship

This is the part where NQA already has more practice than most new archives, because
much of it is **built into the software**, not just aspired to. The principles below
point at things we actually do.

### Consent

- **Consent is tracked on every record**, not assumed. Each entry carries a
  `consent_status` (`not required` / `pending` / `granted` / `restricted`) and a
  record of *how* it entered the archive (`provenance`).
- **A record with `pending` or `restricted` consent cannot be published** — the
  software enforces this, so an un-consented record can't slip out by accident.
- **Living people get extra care.** Public roles and public advocacy can be
  documented from cited sources; private personal history is held back pending a
  courtesy contact and consent. (We already do this for specific individuals.)
- **The right to be removed or corrected.** Anyone featured can ask us to correct,
  restrict, or remove material about them. We honour reasonable requests. (See §5 for
  process.)

### Sources & accuracy

- **Direct sources only. We never invent** facts, dates, names, or quotes. If a
  detail isn't in a source, it's flagged as needing confirmation — not published as
  fact.
- **A human publishes.** New entries are created as drafts; a person reviews and
  chooses to publish. Nothing auto-publishes.
- **Staff notes stay private.** Working notes and uncertainties live in protected
  fields, not in the public record.

### Privacy

- **We withhold personal contact details** — private emails, phone numbers, home
  addresses — even when the person is otherwise public.
- **We are careful with the dead and with those who can't consent** (e.g. we do not
  name someone's family members who aren't themselves public, without their consent).

### Data — collection, storage, dissemination

- **What's public vs. private.** Full preservation text (scraped or pasted source
  material) is stored *privately* by default and is never shown publicly unless it's
  explicitly marked public. Public pages show our catalogued entry, not raw dumps.
- **Where the data lives.** The archive runs on WordPress hosted at [hosting], with
  production as the single source of truth. *(To fill in for the group: who has
  access, where backups go, how often, and who holds the keys if the current steward
  is unavailable.)*
- **Preservation.** We capture source liveness and Wayback Machine snapshots so that
  citations don't rot.
- **Dissemination.** We link back to original sources and cite them. We stay within
  the Niagara-masthead scope and don't republish full copyrighted articles — we
  preserve privately and point publicly.

> **Open questions:** Where should backups live so the archive survives the loss of
> any one account or person? Who, besides the current steward, should hold
> credentials? Do we want a written takedown/correction policy published on the site?

### AI usage (stated plainly, because it's true)

The archive was built with substantial help from an AI assistant, and honesty about
that is part of stewarding it well. Our stance:

- **AI is a tool for research, drafting, and code — never a source of truth.** It
  does not generate history. Every fact in the archive traces to a real, cited
  source, not to a model.
- **Humans stay in control of the record.** AI never decides consent, never
  publishes, and never sets a record live. A person reviews everything.
- **No fabrication, ever.** The "never invent" rule (§3) applies with full force to
  anything AI touches; AI-suggested detail without a source is treated as a lead to
  verify, not a fact.
- **Transparency.** We're willing to say publicly and to contributors that we use AI
  tools, and how. People trusting us with their stories deserve to know.

> **Open question:** How much of this do we want to say *on the site* (an "About our
> methods" note), versus keep as internal practice?

### Land, Indigenous, and Two-Spirit stewardship

- We work on the traditional territory of the Haudenosaunee and Anishinaabe peoples,
  and we hold a responsibility to honour Indigenous histories and peoples alongside
  queer ones.
- We are committed to including **Two-Spirit and Indigenous queer voices** — and to
  doing so in relationship, not extraction. Where we document Two-Spirit history, we
  seek to do it *with* the relevant communities and organizations (e.g. Fort Erie
  Native Friendship Centre), not simply *about* them.

> **Open question:** Our land acknowledgement is currently marked "pending community
> review." Who reviews it, and with whom do we build the relationships to do the
> Two-Spirit work properly?

## 4. Decision-Making

### Where we are honestly starting from

Right now this is effectively a solo project with a small circle of trusted
contributors. That's fine as a starting point and unsustainable as an ending point.
The question isn't whether to change it, but how.

### Structure options (to decide together)

- **A. Solo steward + advisors.** One person makes decisions, consults a few
  trusted people. *Simple; fast; fragile; not very accountable. Bad.*
- **B. Working group / collective.** A small group shares decisions, ideally
  reflecting the community's diversity. *More accountable and resilient; slower;
  needs light structure to avoid drift.*
- **C. Incorporate as a non-profit.** Formal governance, board, ability to hold
  funds and apply for grants. *Most durable and fundable; most administrative
  overhead; can create its own kind of closed-board problem if not done carefully.*

> **Open question:** Which of these are we aiming for, and what's the *next step* from
> where we are — even if the destination is C, the next move might be B.

### How decisions get made (proposed default)

- **Day-to-day** curation and cataloguing: whoever is doing the work decides, within
  the principles in this document.
- **Significant** decisions — publishing a sensitive record, accepting a major
  donation, partnering with or endorsing another organization, spending money,
  changing these principles — go to the group, with an effort to reach consensus and
  a clear fallback if we can't.
- **Anything touching consent or a living person's private history** always gets a
  second set of eyes.

### The endorsement / partnership test

We *will* get asked to lend the archive's name, presence, or endorsement — to
present somewhere, co-sign a statement, partner on an event. Because the archive
carries a kind of moral authority ("the queer history people said yes"), these
aren't neutral. The worked example Steph raised — *an invitation to present at an
annual Niagara police / "gay cop" event* — is exactly the kind of request that needs
a lens, given how contested the relationship between queer communities and police is,
and how much that history is *part of what we document*.

We don't pre-decide that example here. Instead, a request gets weighed against:

1. **Does it serve the community the archive is accountable to** — or mainly serve
   the requester's image?
2. **Who might be harmed or alienated** by our name being attached, especially the
   most marginalized parts of our community?
3. **Is it consistent with what the archive documents** — including histories of
   harm?
4. **Are we being asked to *contribute history*, or to *provide cover*?** The first
   is our job; the second isn't.
5. **Can we say yes with conditions** (e.g. present the history honestly, including
   the hard parts) rather than a flat yes/no?

Significant endorsement/partnership requests go to the group (§4), not one person.

## 5. Conflict & Concerns

Maintaining an archive isn't especially prone to conflict — but when something
contentious *does* come up (a disputed fact, a removal request, an interpersonal
issue, a hard endorsement ask), having even a light process keeps it from becoming
personal or stuck.

### A light process

1. **Raise it.** Anyone — contributor, community member, or team — can raise a
   concern, through the contact inbox or directly. We acknowledge receipt promptly.
2. **Pause if needed.** If a *published* record is in dispute or a consent question is
   open, we can temporarily unpublish/restrict it while we work it out. Better to be
   briefly invisible than wrongly public.
3. **Talk it through.** The relevant people discuss, in good faith, aiming for
   agreement. Bring in the wider group for significant matters (§4).
4. **Decide and record.** We make a decision and note *why* — so the reasoning
   survives and we're consistent next time.
5. **Escalate to community when warranted.** For decisions that genuinely affect the
   community's trust, we consult beyond the team.

### Specific kinds of concern

- **Factual dispute:** re-check the source. If the source is wrong or thin, correct
  or flag it. We change the record to match the evidence, not the loudest voice.
- **Removal / restriction request from someone featured:** default toward honouring
  it for private individuals; weigh public-interest documentation for public figures
  and public acts; err on the side of the person's dignity when unsure.
- **Interpersonal conflict on the team:** address directly and early; don't let the
  work absorb it.
- **A request that splits the group** (like the endorsement example): use the §4 test
  and default to consensus; if we can't agree, default to *not* attaching the
  archive's name.
